Global child mortality (deaths before age five) has fallen by roughly 60
percent since 1990, from about 93 deaths per 1,000 live births to about 37 per
1,000 by the early 2020s (UN Inter-agency Group for Child Mortality Estimation,
"Levels and Trends in Child Mortality 2023"; WHO child mortality fact sheet).

Three of the largest contributors:

1. Expanded immunization. Wider coverage of vaccines against measles, diphtheria,
   tetanus, pertussis, and Haemophilus influenzae type b cut deaths from
   vaccine-preventable disease (WHO, "Immunization coverage" fact sheet, 2023).

2. Treatment of diarrheal disease and pneumonia. Oral rehydration therapy and
   broader access to antibiotics and care reduced mortality from the two
   leading infectious killers of young children (UNICEF, "Diarrhoeal disease"
   and "Pneumonia" data pages, 2023).

3. Better maternal and newborn care and nutrition. Skilled birth attendance,
   improved nutrition, and reductions in stunting lowered neonatal and
   nutrition-related deaths (UNICEF/WHO joint malnutrition estimates, 2023).

Attribution of the exact share of the decline to any single factor is
uncertain, since these drivers overlap and vary by region, so the three above
are best read as leading contributors rather than a precise ranking.
